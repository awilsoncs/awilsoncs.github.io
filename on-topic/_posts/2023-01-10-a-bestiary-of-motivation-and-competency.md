# A Bestiary of Motivation and Competency

A daring knight wanders into your kingdom, armed and ready to unseat everything you've built. You (the _evil_ lord of this domain) must send your monsters to defend your stake, lest it is lost.

First, you send the Zombie, whose sluggish blows are no match for the knight's tough armor. The Zombie fails to muster any zeal, and the Knight quickly dispatches it.

Next, you send the eager Goblin. The Goblin cackles and rushes off. However, you quickly realize The Goblin is burning down a nearby village instead of dealing with the Knight.

Third, you send in the gruff Troll. The Knight stands no chance of getting through the Troll, but the Troll refuses to leave its bridge, so the Knight finds another way into your domain.

Finally, as a last resort, you appeal to the Dragon. The Dragon has terrifying power and is maniacal in purpose. It revels at the chance to defeat a Knight and quickly rises to the challenge before skillfully eliminating the threat.

The members of your team fall into these four categories. In this post, we'll look at how to determine an individual's monster and how you should approach coaching them.

## The Two Axes: Motivation and Competency

Let us properly define two axes. I won't dally long on these, but we need a common vocabulary.

The first axis is motivation. Poorly motivated individuals waffle on decisions, fail to deliver, and leech energy from the organization. Highly motivated individuals exhibit curiosity, propose possibilities, and inspire others. Focus on their willingness to engage with problems and to remain positive in the face of complexity.

The second axis is competency, as measured strictly by the quality of their outcomes. This measure is a proxy for technical approach, knowledge, and processes, but you should not consider those instead. Focus on their ability to deliver results in a professional manner.

## The Four Monsters

Given these two axes, we can visualize a matrix. The quadrants are associated with a monster whose character helps to determine how an individual fits into the scheme. The monsters are easy to remember, which makes this system valuable when snap judgments are necessary.

### The Goblin (High Motivation - Low Competence)

The Goblin brings a high level of energy to a team. They take on problems beyond their comfort zone and quickly jump on new challenges and opportunities. So what's the problem?

Goblins bite off more than they can chew. They embark on enormous refactors without a plan. They take mental shortcuts instead of putting in the work to solve complex problems. They cause the team to clean up after them. They chase shiny objects. Goblins may also have arrived at their current situation by the [Peter Principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_principle), i.e., the company promoted them into a position in which they needed to be more competent.

The easiest way to detect a Goblin is to imagine that you have a whole warren of them- everyone on the team is a clone of this individual. With a warren, you'll likely end up with a malformed, unworking mess.

> A software development team is tasked with building a new feature for their product. One team member, let's call him John, is a classic Goblin. He jumps at the opportunity to lead the development of the new feature and quickly begins working on it. However, instead of consulting with the team or creating a detailed plan, he starts making significant changes to the codebase without considering the long-term impact or discussing it with the team. He is convinced his approach is the best, but it causes several bugs and breaks existing functionality. The team is forced to spend extra time and resources fixing the issues caused by John's shortcuts. They also need to redo some of the work he had done, as it does not align with the overall architecture of the project. Meanwhile, John becomes frustrated with the constant feedback and starts ignoring it, believing that the team does not understand his vision.

Even in life-and-death situations, individuals must stop, assess, decide, and act. The Goblin should focus on the first two until they're comfortable moving through the stages. Here are some techniques to consider:

**Focus on critical feedback and competence development.** The Goblin is willing to receive feedback because they're eager to grow. Don't hold back; make sure their leaders push on them to produce ever-better work. Lean into professional development initiatives and express the benefits of these programs to your Goblins.

**Align them with their competence.** Goblins aren't necessarily Goblins in every arena. If they have outstanding skills– just not the ones the team needs– consider moving them into a role that better suits them. With that said, only take this action if you're confident about their eagerness to learn the new position. You

**Create a safe environment and let them deal with it.** One great way for people to learn is to let them make mistakes, observe the issues they caused, then have them deal with the outcome. [The Code Whisperer wrote a great post on letting individuals overengineer to help them learn design wisdom](https://blog.thecodewhisperer.com/permalink/investing-wisely-in-architecture-through-refactoring). The key here is that the issues caused are small enough that individuals can improve them later. Keep your hand on the handbrake so your Goblins don't make an irreversible mistake.

> John's skills align well with software development, but he needs to improve to be a project lead. He also lacks leadership and teamwork. By identifying specific areas of improvement and addressing those directly, the manager can focus John's energy on self-improvement. Additionally, the manager assigns a smaller project but gives John the explicit goal of design and delegation rather than implementation. By creating some structure around John's weak areas, his manager can create a safe environment to build leadership and teamwork.

### The Troll (Low Motivation - High Competence)

Trolls are guardians of the path. They keep a close eye on everything, preventing low-quality work from passing. They identify edge cases and raise concerns. Trolls sound like ideal colleagues!

Trolls pose a problem opposite from Goblins. They work too conservatively, accounting for myriad possibilities and dreading worst-case scenarios. They slow the team down by complaining, criticizing, or condemning plans without providing any forward motion.

> One of your team members, Bill, is a senior developer and highly competent, but he also tends to be negative and critical of new ideas. The team receives a new project that requires a significant amount of code refactoring. Bill immediately starts raising concerns. He points out all the edge cases and potential issues that could arise during the refactoring process. He's worried that the new code might introduce bugs and that the refactoring process might be too risky. He says the deadline is too tight, and the project will likely take longer. He also suggests that it is much safer to leave the codebase as is.

There are a few tactics a leader may take:

**Dig into their intrinsic motivations (I like [CHAMPFROGS](https://management30.com/practice/moving-motivators/).** Every manager and leader should know their team's intrinsic motivations. Have them enumerate how the project could improve things they care about. You can help them climb over pessimism by focusing on an optimistic outcome.

**Coach hard on negativity.** Listen closely to every Troll interaction. Do not let a public instance of FUD (fear, uncertainty, and doubt) slip by without coaching. Complaints, criticism, and condemnation should be isolated to one-on-ones up the chain and never to the Troll's mentees or junior coworkers.

**Let them contribute to stability.** Invite the Troll to do what they do best, but in a productive way. Teams should log risks and concerns formally, so let the Troll run the risk register. Coach them on gathering consensus, and probe their philosophies for acceptable tradeoffs between risk and delivery.

Since Trolls tend to be senior team members, motivating them should be a top priority. Pessimism in their position can lead to fear, uncertainty, and doubt. Optimism will lead to impact and improvements.

> As the project progresses, Bill's team lead coaches him on negativity through one-on-ones, and it eventually becomes apparent that Bill worries about the potential reputation impacts he may incur if the refactor leads to security issues. With that knowledge, Bill's lead asks him to focus his energy on developing the security plan for the new technology. Bill feels energized because he's assigned a project component that closely aligns with his values.

### The Zombie (Low Motivation - Low Competence)

A Zombie bears a faint resemblance to the person you need. They remind you of a great past, and you may see the brilliance in their interactions and ambitions. However, Zombies are neither highly motivated nor produce quality work for their level and role. Zombies are a type where Performance-Potential matrices have failed me in the past, whereas the Quality-Motivation Bestiary does not.

A Zombie is easy to identify. They miss deadlines; they take required, minimal action without considering broader strategy. They remain in the background during team events. There's always some tiny reason why things don't go according to plan with Zombies, which can occasionally make them hard to deal with.

> One of your team members, Janice, has been assigned to lead the project's testing and quality assurance portion. However, you've noticed that Janice is not performing up to expectations. She consistently misses deadlines, delivers low-quality work, and does not actively participate in team meetings. Furthermore, she is not motivated and does not consider the broader strategy of the project.

The first thing to know is that the problem does not lie with the Zombie. The Zombie may be a charitable soul, a disciplined worker- just not for this job. The problem lies with you. Abolish hope and pursue these actions:

**Seek remediation immediately.** As soon as a whiff of decay enters your mind, initiate your organization's performance improvement process. This statement may be controversial, but HR departments are more likely to serve as friction than grease. Nearly every organization is willing to believe in a well-managed turnaround should it occur.

**Probe for burnout.** An otherwise promising worker may be suffering from burnout. Look for the signs, and ask about their relationship to work. If they show signs of burnout after a history of solid performance, ignore their rationalizations. Initiate burnout remediation as soon as possible.

**Document everything.** Make sure to tally work completed, results, discussion participation, reviews, etc. Maintain this information compared to an individual of similar level and role.

**Seek the root of your curse.** In every case, something has gone wrong in the organization when a Zombie arises. Find one source of the problem and work proactively to eliminate it.

> During your investigations, you notice that Janice suffers from burnout. She has a solid performance history, but now she seems disengaged and unenthusiastic about her work. You speak with her and find out that she's been going through some personal issues that have been affecting her work. By initiating burnout procedures and documenting your conversations along the way, Janice is able to turn her performance around.

The key to remember is that it may or may not be worth your time resurrecting a Zombie. Consider whether they previously demonstrated a high talent level and are now motivated to resolve the issue. If the answer to either of these is "no," it may be better to let them go.

### The Dragon (High Motivation - High Competence)

The Dragon exhibits strong competence and motivation for their role and level. They serve as an unstoppable force within their domain. Dragons find themselves in positions of leadership due to these qualities. Dragons are excellent to have at your disposal.

However, Dragons often find their positions to be unsatisfying. A caged Dragon is more of a problem than a boon.

> Sarah is leading the development efforts for a project. Despite her high motivation and competence, Sarah finds it difficult to work with others because they sometimes fail to live up to her expectations. She believes she can tackle the entire project independently. Her approach is causing some tension within the team and delays in the project.

**Focus on teamwork.** The most significant risk with Dragons is that they struggle to work with others. They often believe they can tackle the entire project independently and don't realize they have limits until it's too late. Encourage them into positions of leadership by focusing on their coaching practice and requiring that they practice patience. Have them lead a [What's not to like about this code?](https://gist.github.com/xpepper/3c604f6fb0fe731a8d8281a5301c71a0) session to help them separate strong preferences from requirements.

**Turn them loose.** A rampaging Dragon can burn down a countryside worth of problems. See where their intuition takes them. Grant them a level of autonomy slightly higher than the norm for their position. Encourage Dragons to take a few hours weekly for freeform or exploratory work. At a junior level, this will help them learn new things, and at a senior level, it will uncover problems that may have flown under their leaders' radar.

**Encourage them to branch out.** A Dragon is respected in the organization, which positions them well to impact the culture positively. Encourage them to lead talks and work with other teams to build connections. A Dragon is mature enough to gain context at a level higher than their peers. Invite them to higher-level sessions and communications that lend to them gaining a better view of the business landscape.

**Focus on their next steps.** Dragons are talented and ambitious. If you aren't talking about the next steps with them, someone will. As soon as you know you have a Dragon on your hands, begin planning for a promotion. Understand their career goals and values. Regularly discuss their development.

> Sarah's manager expands her domain to include weekly design reviews, brown bag sessions, and half a day of exploratory time per week. With these broader responsibilities, Sarah can no longer solve every problem directly. Her manager encourages her to focus on mentoring and delegation, and by the end of the quarter, she is ready for a promotion to Staff Engineer.

By broadening a Dragon's scope and reinforcing their teamwork, you will elevate ambitious employees to provide even more value to your organization.

## Caveats

It's important to remember that, in real life, people are not strictly an archetype, they are complex and multidimensional, and their behavior can vary depending on the situation, their emotions, workload, and personal matters. Additionally, people can change, develop and grow over time.

You may have Goblin-Dragons and Zombie-Trolls on your team. You may even have Gorgons, Minotaurs, and Banshees. The most important part of this structure is considering it a starting framework, a suggestion. The real people you lead will require nuance and understanding to coach.

## Conclusion

The people you coach are all unique and bring their strengths and weaknesses to the table. This problem can make evaluating them difficult. In this post, we looked at four quadrants of a Competency-Motivation matrix and compared them to fantasy Monsters. This narrative allows a coach to assess the needs of an individual quickly. Given this assessment, coaches may apply established strategies for developing the individual further.
